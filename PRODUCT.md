# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Children aged 6 to 8 exploring stories autonomously at home, in a library, or in a classroom corner without needing an adult to operate the interface. Secondary users are teachers, librarians, and families who want a reliable archive of the original "Historia do Dia" material.

## Product Purpose

Reconstruct historiadodia.pt as a heritage-first pedagogical website. The product preserves as much original content as possible from Wayback Machine snapshots, then presents it through a modern reading and listening experience.

Success means a child can open the story for the current calendar day, read it or hear it read aloud, and browse stories from other days. The archive must make provenance clear: original text, original illustration, original audio, recovered PDF, rerecorded narration, editorial adaptation, and missing material are all labelled distinctly.

## Brand Personality

Warm, tactile, and gently enchanted.

The interface should feel like a paper reading corner built around recovered illustrations, not like a generic children's app. It should be calm enough for early readers, but still carry a sense of discovery through the calendar and archive.

## Anti-references

Do not create a generic cartoon interface, mascot-led world, or gamified points system. Do not make the archive feel like a serious museum catalogue that children cannot use. Do not invent visual material that competes with the recovered original illustrations. Do not hide gaps in the archive or present rerecorded narration as original audio. Do not depend on animation for core comprehension.

## Design Principles

1. Put recovered material first: original illustrations, icons, PDFs, text, and audio are the visual and editorial anchors.
2. Mark provenance honestly: every story state should tell users what was recovered, rerecorded, adapted, or missing.
3. Make the day obvious: the current day's story is the primary entry point, with archive exploration close behind.
4. Support early readers: large controls, comfortable line length, simple labels, and listening support matter more than dense navigation.
5. Keep wonder quiet: use warmth, paper texture, and small moments of discovery without overwhelming the reading task.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA. Prioritize keyboard navigation, visible focus, reduced motion support, high contrast text on tinted paper backgrounds, large hit targets, captions or transcripts for audio, and clear distinction between original and rerecorded audio. The experience must remain usable without sound.
